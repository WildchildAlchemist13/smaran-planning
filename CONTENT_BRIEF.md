# 🎨 PART 1 — DEITY ART (Artists)

## Why this is the most important investment

Original commissioned art is the **single moat** that can't be cloned by AI or competitors. Every existing Hindu app uses scraped Google images or AI slop. We win on visual reverence.

**Total commitment**: 30 illustrations to launch, 365 over year 1.
**Trial commission**: 1 illustration each from 3–5 artists, paid (₹3,000–5,000 each).
**Full commission rate target**: ₹3,000–6,000 per illustration depending on complexity and artist tier.

## Trial commission spec (send after artist agrees)

**Philosophical lane**: **Mainstream Vedanta** (Vivekananda / Chinmaya / Easwaran-influenced — accessible, universalist, non-sectarian).

## Where to find scholars / Gita teachers

### Institutions with strong Vedanta lineages
- **Chinmaya Mission** (chinmayamission.com) — most accessible modern Vedanta tradition; many trained acharyas open to consulting work
- **Ramakrishna Mission / Belur Math** — Vivekananda lineage; scholarly + accessible
- **Arsha Vidya Gurukulam** (Coimbatore + USA) — Swami Dayananda's tradition, very rigorous
- **Vedanta Society** chapters worldwide
- **Kanchi Math** — traditional, more orthodox

### University Sanskrit / Indology departments
- BHU (Banaras Hindu University)
- JNU School of Sanskrit Studies
- Karnataka Sanskrit University
- University of Madras Sanskrit dept
- Many young PhDs with both traditional + modern training, looking for paid scholarly work

### Specific scholar profiles to look for on LinkedIn / Twitter / YouTube
- "Vedanta acharya" / "Gita teacher" + India location
- YouTube channels with 5k–50k subs teaching Gita weekly (these scholars often have day jobs and are open to commission work)
- Authors of recently published Gita commentaries (last 5 years) — ground-up search on Amazon India

### Outreach tip
Avoid the celebrity teachers (Sadhguru, Gaur Gopal Das) — too expensive, not available. Target the **next tier**: 30–55 year olds with serious training, ~10k YouTube subs, written 1–2 books, active on Instagram occasionally.

## Trial commission spec

### Deliverable per verse
- 250–300 words
- Format:
  ```
  Chapter [N], Verse [N]
  
  [Sanskrit Devanagari]
  [Roman transliteration with diacritics]
  [English translation]
  
  [Commentary — 200–250 words]
  [Reflection prompt — 1 sentence at end]
  ```

### Tone requirements
- Reverent but not preachy
- Translates ancient wisdom to modern situations (work, anxiety, relationships, purpose, grief)
- Uses examples from contemporary life occasionally
- Cites the verse + Sanskrit, never just paraphrases
- Closes with a single reflection question or actionable nudge

### What to avoid
- Sectarian language ("our path is the only true path")
- Academic jargon (no "epistemological framework" — talk to a thoughtful 30-year-old, not a PhD committee)
- Overly long Sanskrit philosophical detours
- Western-pop-spirituality framing ("manifest your best life")

### Trial verses (these three are deliberately chosen)
1. **Gita 2.47** — *karmaṇy-evādhikāras te* — most-quoted verse; tests handling of nishkama karma
2. **Gita 2.55** — *prajahāti yadā kāmān* — the sthita-prajna verse; tests handling of detachment
3. **Gita 6.5** — *uddhared ātmanātmānaṁ* — self-mastery; tests handling of inner conflict

## Translation source decision

Locked decision needed before full commission:

- **Option A**: Commission scholar to write fresh original translation (₹15,000–30,000 add-on, full IP ownership)
- **Option B**: Use public domain — Edwin Arnold (1885) or Sacred Books of the East series (free, no licensing risk, but Victorian English may need light modernization)
- **Option C**: License a modern translation (Easwaran, Sargeant, Mascaró) — usually requires negotiation, ₹50k–2L, ongoing royalty risk

**Recommended**: Option A. Cleanest IP position, scholar gets meaningful commission, avoids Victorian-English friction. Decide with scholar in Week 0.

# 🎙 PART 3 — SANSKRIT RECITATION (Recorded by Rev's mother)

> **Decision D12**: Rev's mother will record all Sanskrit chants. This becomes both a cost saver and a brand story. Professional reciter sourcing is no longer needed.

## What we need

- All 700 Gita verses in **Sanskrit** (chant style — clean, devotional, 30–60 sec per verse) — recorded by Rev's mother at home
- Same 700 verses in **English narration** (warm, calm, reverent — 60–90 sec per verse) — still commissioned externally OR voice-cloned with consent

**Equipment one-time cost**: ~₹8–12k (mic, pop filter, software)
**Phasing**: 50–100 verses for launch; rolling additions over 2–4 months

---

## Home recording setup (one-time, ~₹8–12k)

### Equipment to buy
| Item | Recommendation | Approx. cost |
|---|---|---|
| USB condenser microphone | Maono PD400X (₹8k) OR Rode NT-USB Mini (₹10k) OR Blue Yeti Nano (₹12k) | ₹8–12k |
| Pop filter | Generic foam or mesh | ₹300–500 |
| Mic stand (desktop) | Generic | ₹500–800 |
| Recording software | Audacity (free) OR GarageBand (free on Mac) | ₹0 |
| Optional: acoustic foam panels | For room treatment if echo is an issue | ₹2–3k |

**Total**: ₹10–15k worst case. Budget ₹15k to be safe.

### Recording environment tips
- Quiet room with soft furnishings (carpet, curtains, sofa) — reduces echo
- Record in early mornings or late evenings (less ambient noise)
- Distance: 6–10 inches from mic
- Sit upright; warm up voice with a few oms before starting
- Aim for 5–7 verses per session (max 30 min) — voice fatigue is real

### File specs
- Format: WAV (uncompressed) or 320kbps MP3
- Sample rate: 44.1 kHz
- Mono is fine; stereo unnecessary for vocal-only chant
- One file per verse, named: `gita_[chapter]_[verse].wav` (e.g., `gita_2_47.wav`)

---

## Recording schedule

### Realistic pace
- 5–7 verses per session × 2–3 sessions per week = 10–20 verses/week
- 700 verses ÷ 15/week average = **~10–12 weeks** for full set
- For launch: target **50–100 most-important verses** in first 4 weeks

### Priority order for recording (so launch isn't blocked)
1. **Week 0–1**: 30 most-important verses (deity-day-aligned launch set) — see scholar's curated list
2. **Week 2–4**: Next 50 verses
3. **Week 5–12**: Remaining ~600 verses (rolled out as in-app updates after launch)

This way the app launches with full audio for the first 30–50 days of content, and the rest fills in over time as content is added daily.

---

## Workflow for each batch

1. **Mom records** 5–7 verses in one session
2. **Rev or audio editor** does light cleanup in Audacity:
   - Noise reduction (1-click filter)
   - Trim silence at start/end
   - Normalize volume to consistent level
3. **Scholar reviews** the batch (check pronunciation accuracy) — 10 min per batch
4. **Files uploaded** to Supabase Storage / CDN with verse-ID naming
5. **App pulls** the audio file matching today's verse

**Audio editing time per session**: ~15 min if Rev does it himself, or ~₹500/session if outsourced to a freelance editor on Fiverr.

## Where to find a Sanskrit reciter

### Best sources
- **Veda Pathshalas** — students or teachers from Vedic schools (look up Krishna Yajur Veda or Rig Veda pathshalas in Andhra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala)
- **ISKCON gurukulas** — many trained reciters (note: their style is devotional, may not match Vedanta lane perfectly)
- **Vedic chanting teachers on YouTube** — many teach online; some open to commission audio work
- **Sanskrit university students** — BHU, Karnataka Sanskrit University, JNU
- **Carnatic music vocalists** — some specialize in Sanskrit shloka recitation (combines musical training with correct pronunciation)
- **Voiceover marketplaces** — voquent.com, voices.com — search "Sanskrit", "Hindu chant"

### What to listen for in samples
- **Correct pronunciation** of all Sanskrit phonemes (especially retroflex consonants — ट, ठ, ड, ढ, ण — and aspirate vs unaspirate distinctions)
- **Calm pacing** — not rushed; not so slow it loses energy
- **Devotional but not theatrical** — clean reverence, no melodramatic intonation
- **Clear voice** — no background noise, professional mic preferred

## Where to find an English narrator

- Voquent / Voices.com — Indian-English voice actors with a calm/spiritual register
- Local studios in Bangalore, Mumbai — recommend Tasveer Sound (BLR), Aural Audio Studios (BOM)
- Audiobook narrators with spiritual/wellness portfolio

### Vocal qualities for English narration
- Warm, calm, slightly low register
- Indian-English accent preferred (cultural authenticity for Indian users; diaspora users find it familiar)
- Not "yoga-instructor breathy" — more "wise friend reading aloud"
